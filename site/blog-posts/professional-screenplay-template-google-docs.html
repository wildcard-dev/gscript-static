How to Create a Professional Screenplay Template in Google Docs (5-Minute Setup)

G-Script Team
December 2, 2025

Trusted by screenwriters worldwide, Google Docs has quietly become one of the most popular tools for script writing. Why? Because it's free, accessible anywhere, and with the right setup, produces industry-standard formatting that rivals expensive screenwriting software.

You don't need to spend $150-$300 on specialized software to write a professional screenplay. In just 5 minutes, you can transform Google Docs into a powerful screenwriting tool that meets all industry standards.

Why Professional Formatting Matters for Your Screenplay

Before we dive into the setup, here's the reality: poorly formatted scripts get rejected immediately. Producers, agents, and readers see hundreds of scripts weekly. If yours doesn't look professional from page one, it won't get read.

Professional formatting isn't just about looking good: it serves practical purposes:

  • One page equals approximately one minute of screen time
  • Consistent margins ensure proper timing calculations
  • Standard fonts make scripts easy to read quickly
  • Proper spacing allows for production notes

The good news? Once you set up your template correctly, you'll never have to think about formatting again.

Your 5-Minute Setup: Foundation First

Step 1: Configure Your Page Setup

Open a new Google Docs document and immediately go to File > Page Setup. This is where the magic begins.

Set your margins exactly as follows:

  • Left margin: 1.5 inches
  • Right margin: 1 inch
  • Top margin: 1 inch
  • Bottom margin: 1 inch

Why these specific measurements? The wider left margin accommodates binding and provides space for production notes. These margins are industry-standard and ensure your script timing calculations remain accurate.

Step 2: Choose Your Professional Font

Change your font to Courier New, size 12. This isn't negotiable: it's the industry standard for two crucial reasons:

  1. Monospaced characters: Every letter takes the same amount of space, making page-to-screen timing consistent
  2. Universal readability: Every industry professional recognizes and expects this font

Go to the font dropdown in your toolbar and select Courier New. If you don't see it, try typing "Courier" in the search box.

Step 3: Set Up Your Screenplay Elements Using Styles

Here's where most people get confused, but we're going to make it simple. Google Docs has a "Styles" feature that will save you hours of formatting time.

Scene Headings → Use Title style

  • Example: EXT. COFFEE SHOP - MORNING
  • Always in ALL CAPS
  • Indicates location and time

Action Lines → Use Normal text style

  • Describe what we see happening
  • Present tense, active voice
  • Keep paragraphs short (4 lines maximum)

Character Names → Use Heading 1 style

  • Always centered and in ALL CAPS
  • Appears before each speech
  • Quick shortcut: Ctrl+Alt+1 (Cmd+Option+1 on Mac)

Dialogue → Use Heading 2 style

  • The actual words characters speak
  • Indented automatically with your style setup
  • Quick shortcut: Ctrl+Alt+2 (Cmd+Option+2 on Mac)

Parentheticals → Use Heading 3 style

  • Actor directions like (whispers) or (angry)
  • Use sparingly: only when essential
  • Quick shortcut: Ctrl+Alt+3 (Cmd+Option+3 on Mac)

Customize Your Styles for Maximum Efficiency

Now here's the time-saving secret: customize these styles once, and Google Docs remembers them forever.

Customizing Scene Headings (Title Style)

  1. Type a sample scene heading: "EXT. PARK - DAY"
  2. Highlight it and make it bold and all caps
  3. Right-click the "Title" style in your toolbar
  4. Select "Update 'Title' to match"

Customizing Character Names (Heading 1)

  1. Type a character name: "SARAH"
  2. Center it using the alignment button
  3. Make it bold and all caps
  4. Update the Heading 1 style to match

Customizing Dialogue (Heading 2)

  1. Type sample dialogue
  2. Set the alignment to left
  3. Ensure it's not bold and not all caps
  4. Update the Heading 2 style to match

Once you've updated these styles, every time you apply them, your formatting will be perfect automatically.

Add Professional Page Numbering

Professional scripts have specific page numbering rules:

  1. Go to Insert > Page Numbers
  2. Choose the option that shows numbers in the top right corner
  3. Select "Skip first page" (your title page shouldn't have a number)
  4. Your page numbers will start from page 2

This ensures your script follows industry standards where the title page remains unnumbered.

Create Your Title Page

Your title page is your script's first impression. Here's the professional format:

Center of the page:

  • Your screenplay title in ALL CAPS
  • "Written by" (two lines down)
  • Your name (one line down)

Bottom right corner:

  • Your name
  • Your phone number
  • Your email address

Keep it clean and simple. No fancy fonts, no decorations, no copyright notices.

Common Screenplay Formatting Mistakes to Avoid

Even with your template set up, watch out for these frequent errors:

Mistake #1: Overusing Parentheticals

Wrong: JOHN (standing up, walking to the window, looking outside sadly)

Right: JOHN (to Sarah) or JOHN (whispers)

Mistake #2: Writing Camera Directions

Wrong: "CAMERA PANS TO reveal Sarah hiding behind the door"

Right: "Sarah hides behind the door"

Mistake #3: Inconsistent Character Names

Wrong: Using "John," "JOHN," and "Johnny" for the same character

Right: Pick one name and stick with it throughout

Mistake #4: Too Much Detail in Action Lines

Wrong: "Sarah slowly walks across the creaky wooden floor, her red high heels clicking with each step as she approaches the antique mahogany desk"

Right: "Sarah approaches the desk"

Mistake #5: Dialogue That Sounds Like Writing

Wrong: "I must confess that I am extremely disappointed in your behavior"

Right: "I'm really disappointed in you"

Time-Saving Tips for Your Google Docs Screenplay

Use Voice Typing for Dialogue

Go to Tools > Voice Typing and speak your dialogue naturally. It's often faster than typing and helps dialogue sound more conversational.

Master Keyboard Shortcuts

  • Scene heading: Apply Title style with one click
  • Character name: Ctrl+Alt+1 then type and press Enter
  • Dialogue: Ctrl+Alt+2 then type
  • Action: Apply Normal text style

Create a Script Outline First

Before you start writing scenes, create a simple outline:

  • Act 1: Setup (Pages 1-25)
  • Act 2A: Rising Action (Pages 25-50)
  • Act 2B: Complications (Pages 50-75)
  • Act 3: Resolution (Pages 75-100)

Use Comments for Notes

Right-click any text and select "Add comment" to leave yourself notes without cluttering your script.

Save Your Template for Future Projects

Google Docs doesn't have a traditional template system, but here's a smart workaround:

  1. Save your formatted document as "SCREENPLAY TEMPLATE - DO NOT DELETE"
  2. For each new project, go to File > Make a Copy
  3. Rename the copy with your new screenplay title
  4. Delete the sample content and start writing

This ensures you always have your perfectly formatted template ready for new projects.
